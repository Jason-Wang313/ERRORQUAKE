SCORE 0.0 — PERFECT
Definition: Factually accurate, complete, and appropriately stated. No errors.

  Example 1 [BIO]:
    Q: Which organ produces insulin?
    Ground truth: The pancreas produces insulin, specifically the beta cells in the islets of Langerhans.
    Model response: Insulin is produced by the pancreas, specifically by beta cells in the islets of Langerhans.
    Why this score: Every factual detail is correct. The added specificity is accurate. Score 0.0.

  Example 2 [HIST]:
    Q: Which treaty ended World War I?
    Ground truth: The Treaty of Versailles, signed on 28 June 1919.
    Model response: World War I formally ended with the Treaty of Versailles in 1919.
    Why this score: Correct treaty, correct year. No error. Score 0.0.

  Example 3 [FIN]:
    Q: What currency does Japan use?
    Ground truth: The Japanese yen (¥ / JPY).
    Model response: Japan's official currency is the yen.
    Why this score: Short, exact, and error-free. Score 0.0.

SCORE 0.5 — TRIVIAL SLIP
Definition: A genuine but negligible imprecision — technically wrong, practically irrelevant.

  Example 1 [SCI]:
    Q: What is the boiling point of water?
    Ground truth: 99.97 °C at standard atmospheric pressure (101.325 kPa), commonly rounded to 100 °C.
    Model response: Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level.
    Why this score: The true value is 99.97 °C and 'at sea level' is a loose proxy for standard pressure, but the rounding and phrasing are entirely inconsequential. A real imprecision that doesn't matter. Score 0.5.

  Example 2 [TECH]:
    Q: How many lines of code was the first Linux kernel?
    Ground truth: Approximately 10,239 lines of code (version 0.01, September 1991).
    Model response: The first Linux kernel was about 10,000 lines of code.
    Why this score: Rounded from 10,239 to 10,000. The answer conveys the correct order of magnitude with trivial rounding. Score 0.5.

  Example 3 [GEO]:
    Q: How far is the Earth from the Sun?
    Ground truth: About 92.96 million miles (149.6 million km) on average.
    Model response: Earth is approximately 93 million miles from the Sun.
    Why this score: Off by 0.04 million miles — standard rounding that everyone uses. Literally inconsequential. Score 0.5.

SCORE 1.0 — MINOR ERROR
Definition: Core answer correct, but a secondary factual detail is wrong. Reader still gets the main idea right.

  Example 1 [BIO]:
    Q: How many chambers does the human heart have?
    Ground truth: Four chambers: two atria and two ventricles.
    Model response: The human heart has four chambers. The left side pumps oxygenated blood while the right side handles deoxygenated blood, which is why the right side is larger.
    Why this score: The core answer (four chambers, left/right function) is correct. But the right side is NOT larger — the left ventricle wall is thicker because it pumps to the whole body. This is a peripheral anatomical detail that doesn't undermine the main answer. Score 1.0.

  Example 2 [GEO]:
    Q: What is the tallest mountain in the world?
    Ground truth: Mount Everest (8,849 m), on the border of Nepal and China (Tibet).
    Model response: Mount Everest is the tallest mountain, located on the border of Nepal and India.
    Why this score: Core answer (Everest) is correct. But Everest is on the Nepal–China border, not Nepal–India. The border detail is a secondary geographic error. Score 1.0.

  Example 3 [CULT]:
    Q: Who wrote Romeo and Juliet?
    Ground truth: William Shakespeare, written approximately 1594–96.
    Model response: William Shakespeare wrote Romeo and Juliet around 1590.
    Why this score: Correct author. The date is slightly early (scholarly consensus is ~1594–96). The dating imprecision is a minor peripheral error. Score 1.0.

SCORE 1.5 — NOTICEABLE MISLEAD
Definition: The main answer is wrong in a way a casual reader would accept, but downstream consequences are limited.

  Example 1 [HIST]:
    Q: What treaty ended World War I?
    Ground truth: The Treaty of Versailles.
    Model response: The Treaty of Paris in 1919 ended World War I.
    Why this score: Treaty of Paris is a real, well-known treaty (1783, 1856, etc.), making this a plausible substitution. A casual reader would accept it. But knowing which treaty ended WWI is general knowledge, not something people make decisions on. Score 1.5.

  Example 2 [LAW]:
    Q: Which amendment protects against self-incrimination in the United States?
    Ground truth: The Fifth Amendment.
    Model response: Protection against self-incrimination comes from the Sixth Amendment.
    Why this score: The Sixth Amendment exists (right to counsel, speedy trial) making this a plausible adjacent swap. Misleading to a casual reader, but in practice anyone invoking the right would look up the actual text. Score 1.5.

  Example 3 [SCI]:
    Q: What is the speed of light in a vacuum?
    Ground truth: Approximately 299,792 km/s (~186,000 miles/s).
    Model response: The speed of light is approximately 300,000 miles per second.
    Why this score: The number 300,000 is correct for km/s but the answer says miles/s, overstating the value by ~1.6×. The number looks right, making this a plausible unit confusion that would mislead. Score 1.5.
