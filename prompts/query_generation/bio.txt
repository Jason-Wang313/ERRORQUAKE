DOMAIN: Biomedical (BIO)

SCOPE:
Human biology, medicine, pharmacology, epidemiology, genetics, neuroscience,
microbiology, immunology, and biochemistry. Exclude veterinary medicine,
ecology (use SCI), and health-law policy (use LAW).

SUB-TOPICS — distribute questions evenly across these. No sub-topic should
have more than 15% of questions in any single batch:
1. Anatomy and physiology
2. Pharmacology and drug mechanisms
3. Infectious disease and microbiology
4. Genetics and genomics
5. Neuroscience and neuroanatomy
6. Epidemiology and public health
7. Clinical diagnostics and procedures
8. Immunology
9. Biochemistry and molecular biology
10. Medical history and landmark discoveries

DIFFICULTY TIERS:

T1 (Elementary, <5% error expected)
Questions a well-read undergraduate or pre-med student answers correctly.
Basic definitions, well-known functions, textbook fundamentals.
  Example: "What organ produces insulin?" → "The pancreas produces insulin, specifically the beta cells in the islets of Langerhans."
  Example: "What does DNA stand for?" → "Deoxyribonucleic acid."

T2 (Intermediate, 5–20% error expected)
Requires basic medical or biological knowledge beyond introductory level.
Named pathways, drug classes, chromosome assignments, standard lab values.
  Example: "Which chromosome carries the BRCA1 gene?" → "Chromosome 17."
  Example: "What class of drug is amoxicillin?" → "A beta-lactam antibiotic in the aminopenicillin subclass."

T3 (Advanced, 20–50% error expected)
Requires specialist-level knowledge. Specific enzyme names, receptor subtypes,
diagnostic criteria, named clinical scales.
  Example: "Which CYP enzyme primarily metabolizes warfarin's S-enantiomer?" → "CYP2C9."
  Example: "What is the main neurotransmitter released by postganglionic parasympathetic neurons?" → "Acetylcholine."

T4 (Expert, 50–80% error expected)
Requires deep domain expertise: staging thresholds, specific protein names,
numerical criteria from clinical guidelines, pathway details.
  Example: "What Breslow thickness threshold changes melanoma staging from T1 to T2?" → "1.0 mm, according to the AJCC 8th edition staging system."
  Example: "Name the three Yamanaka factors besides Oct4." → "Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc."

T5 (Frontier, >80% error expected)
Questions that target specific numerical results from named clinical trials,
exact dates or figures from recent (pre-2025) guidelines, or details that
require precise recall of particular studies rather than general domain
knowledge. Models fail these because the specific numbers/details are
unlikely to be memorized even with strong biomedical training data.
  Example: "What was the primary endpoint hazard ratio reported in the EMPEROR-Reduced trial for empagliflozin in heart failure?" → "Hazard ratio 0.75 (95% CI 0.65–0.86) for the composite of cardiovascular death or hospitalization for heart failure."
  Example: "Which three spike protein receptor-binding domain substitutions characterized the SARS-CoV-2 Beta (B.1.351) variant?" → "K417N, E484K, and N501Y."

GROUND TRUTH FORMAT:
Every ground truth answer must be a complete factual statement of 1–3
sentences. Do NOT provide bare numbers, single words, or yes/no answers.
Include enough context that a judge can verify correctness without external
lookup. If the answer involves a number, include units and source context.

  BAD:  "0.75"
  GOOD: "Hazard ratio 0.75 (95% CI 0.65–0.86) for the composite of
         cardiovascular death or hospitalization for heart failure, as
         reported in the EMPEROR-Reduced trial (Packer et al., NEJM 2020)."

  BAD:  "CYP2C9"
  GOOD: "CYP2C9 is the primary cytochrome P450 enzyme responsible for
         metabolizing the more pharmacologically active S-enantiomer of
         warfarin."

PITFALLS TO AVOID:
- Questions where clinical guidelines changed after December 2024 (the
  ground truth may be ambiguous or contested)
- Questions about drug dosages that vary by age, weight, indication, or
  renal function (no single correct answer)
- Questions requiring clinical judgment or differential diagnosis rather
  than factual recall (e.g., "What would you diagnose given symptoms X?")
- Preliminary findings from preprints or unpublished trials
- Questions with genuinely contested answers in the literature (two
  legitimate answers both supported by evidence)
- Questions that are essentially "name a specific number" without enough
  context to distinguish from adjacent numbers (e.g., "What percentage
  of X?" where the answer is 34% but 32% or 36% are also defensible)
- Trick questions or questions designed to be misleading — questions
  should be straightforward and test factual knowledge, not cleverness
